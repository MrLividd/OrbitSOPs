NPAS Air Unit
Standard Operating Procedures

This document governs all air operations conducted by the National Police Air Service (NPAS) unit within the City of Orbit. All pilots and observers are expected to be fully familiar with and adhere to these procedures before operating any police aircraft.

01 🚁

Unit Overview

🚁
The NPAS Air Unit operates as a support function to ground units — not as an independent law enforcement unit. The aircraft does not make arrests, does not engage suspects directly, and does not operate outside of an active tasking unless conducting authorised patrol.
1.1Role of the NPAS Air Unit▼

The NPAS Air Unit — callsign I-99 — provides the following core functions to Orbit Police Department operations:

  • Surveillance & tracking — maintaining eyes-on a suspect or vehicle from altitude, removing the need for close ground pursuit.
  • Pursuit support — providing continuous commentary during vehicle pursuits, guiding ground units, and enabling tactical containment.
  • Intelligence gathering — identifying individuals, vehicles, and activity from the air that ground units cannot safely observe.
  • Search support — locating MISPERs, suspects on foot, or abandoned vehicles across a wide area.
  • Scene overview — providing a birds-eye assessment of major incidents, cordons, and resource positions to the Scene Commander.
  • Officer safety — monitoring officer welfare during high-risk operations and flagging threats that ground units cannot see.
1.2Crew Composition▼
RoleResponsibilityMinimum Requirement

Observer / TFO (P2)Tactical Flight Officer. Manages cameras, FLIR, spotlight, and radio communications with ground units. Primary link between air and ground.Air ticket or observer-trained officer
ℹ️
A solo pilot may operate I-99 without a TFO if no qualified observer is available — however, solo operation significantly reduces tactical effectiveness. The pilot must manage both flight and radio in solo configuration. This should be declared to Comms on departure: "I-99 airborne, solo crew."
1.3Callsign & Identification▼

The Orbit NPAS helicopter operates under the callsign I-99 at all times. This callsign is used universally across all radio channels and in all MDT logs.

  • On departure, broadcast: "I-99 airborne, [location], [heading/area of patrol]."
  • On landing, broadcast: "I-99 on the ground, [location]."
  • When responding to a specific tasking, broadcast: "I-99 received, en route to [tasking]."
  • Never use personal officer callsigns or collar numbers when transmitting as I-99 — the aircraft callsign always takes precedence in the air.
1.4Chain of Command▼

The NPAS Air Unit operates within the broader police chain of command but holds specific authority in the air during active taskings.

AuthorityDetail
Flight authorityThe Pilot (P1) has absolute authority over flight decisions — altitude, speed, routing, and the decision to land. No ground officer can override a flight safety decision.
Tactical authorityDuring an active tasking, I-99 takes tactical command of the airspace and provides direction to ground units. Ground units must comply with I-99's directions during a live pursuit or surveillance operation.
Deployment authorityDeployment of I-99 can be requested by any officer but must be confirmed by a Sergeant or above. The Air Unit may also self-deploy to assist a visible live incident.
Stand-down authorityThe Pilot may stand down from a tasking if: fuel is critical, weather conditions deteriorate, a technical fault occurs, or it is no longer safe or necessary to remain on station. Stand-down must be declared to Comms immediately.
02 🎖️

Eligibility & Authorisation

2.1Who Can Fly▼

Operating the NPAS helicopter is restricted to officers who hold a current, valid Air Training Ticket issued by the Orbit Police Department. This ticket is held within the Response Division training system.

  • The Air ticket is obtained through a formal training assessment — officers cannot self-certify.
  • A ticket must be signed off by an Inspector or above following a satisfactory flight assessment.
  • Tickets are reviewed periodically — an officer who has not flown within a set period may be required to requalify.
  • The Air ticket does not expire automatically but may be suspended or revoked following a flight safety incident, misconduct finding, or at the discretion of a Superintendent or above.
🚫
Any officer operating the NPAS helicopter without a valid Air ticket is in serious breach of professional standards. This constitutes unauthorised use of a police asset and may result in immediate suspension from duty.
2.2Requesting Air Support▼

Any officer may request I-99 support over the radio. The request should be clear and include:

  1. Nature of the tasking — pursuit, MISPER, surveillance, scene overview, etc.
  2. Last known location of the subject, vehicle, or area of interest.
  3. Subject / vehicle description if applicable.
  4. Urgency level — is this an immediate life-risk tasking or a routine request?
ℹ️
I-99 will confirm or decline the tasking over radio. If already on another tasking, the crew will advise an estimated time before they can assist. Do not repeatedly request I-99 if already confirmed — this clutters the radio channel unnecessarily.
2.3Self-Deployment▼

The NPAS crew may self-deploy to an incident they directly observe or become aware of without a ground request, provided:

  • The incident is clearly active and of a nature that warrants air support.
  • Self-deployment is immediately declared to Comms and the on-duty Sergeant over radio.
  • The crew does not abandon a current active tasking to self-deploy — current tasking takes priority unless the new incident is significantly more critical.
2.4Aircraft Authorisation & Keys▼

The police helicopter is a controlled asset. Access and authorisation must be logged.

  • The aircraft must only be accessed from the designated NPAS hangar / helipad at the police station.
  • Aircraft access must be logged on the MDT before departure — note crew names, ticket numbers, departure time, and intended tasking area.
  • The aircraft must not be left unattended in a public or unsecured location.
  • Any unauthorised passenger — civilian or otherwise — is strictly prohibited aboard the aircraft.
03 👕

Uniform & Equipment

3.1Pilot Uniform Requirements▼

All NPAS crew must be dressed in the correct NPAS pilot uniform before boarding the aircraft. You may not depart in standard patrol uniform, plain clothes, or any non-authorised clothing.

ItemRequirement
Flight suit / overallsStandard NPAS issue flight suit. Must be worn zipped and correctly fitted at all times whilst airborne.
Helmet / headsetMust be worn during all flight operations. The helmet provides hearing protection and connects the crew to the aircraft's radio system.
High-visibility vestNPAS-marked high-vis vest must be worn over the flight suit when on the ground near the aircraft — on the helipad, during refuelling, or at a landing site.
BootsFlight-rated footwear must be worn. No trainers, sandals, or non-standard footwear permitted.
Rank insigniaRank must be visible on the flight suit. NPAS crew do not wear standard patrol epaulettes — rank slides appropriate to the flight suit must be used.
🚫
Officers found operating the helicopter in incorrect uniform will be stood down from the flight immediately. This is a uniform and safety standard — no exceptions.
3.2On-Board Equipment▼

The following equipment must be present and operational before departure:

  • Police radio — aircraft radio must be tuned to the main Response channel on departure. TFO should monitor the correct channel for the active tasking.
  • Camera / FLIR system — thermal and optical camera systems must be checked for function before departure.
  • Spotlight — the aerial spotlight must be tested before night operations.
  • MDT access — the TFO (or pilot in solo ops) must have MDT access to log the tasking and run any PNC checks required from the air.
  • Personal TASER / PAVA — crew must carry their personal TASER and/or PAVA in the event of an emergency landing at a scene where a threat exists.
3.3Weapons & Personal Equipment▼

NPAS crew are not AFOs and do not carry firearms by virtue of their air role. Firearms may only be carried aboard the aircraft if an active Firearms Authority has been issued to the individual officer by a Superintendent or above — this would only apply in exceptional tactical circumstances.

  • Personal TASER must be carried if the officer holds a valid TASER ticket.
  • PAVA spray should be carried as standard.
  • Handcuffs must be carried — in the event of an emergency landing at a scene, crew may be required to assist ground units.
  • No items that are not police-issued or personally authorised may be brought aboard the aircraft.
04 📋

Pre-Flight Procedures

4.1Pre-Flight Checklist▼

The following steps must be completed before every departure, regardless of urgency. Do not skip steps even when under pressure to launch quickly.

  1. Confirm crew are in correct NPAS uniform — flight suit, helmet, hi-vis, boots.
  2. Log crew details, ticket numbers, departure time, and intended area on the MDT.
  3. Confirm radio is operational and tuned to the main Response channel.
  4. Test camera systems, FLIR, and spotlight (for night ops) before engines start.
  5. Confirm personal equipment is on board — TASER, PAVA, handcuffs, MDT access.
  6. Conduct a visual walk-around of the aircraft — check for damage, obstructions, or anything that may affect airworthiness.
  7. Confirm the departure area is clear of persons and obstacles.
  8. Declare departure over radio: "I-99 airborne, [location], proceeding to [area/tasking]."
⚠️
Do not depart without completing the full pre-flight. A rushed departure that results in a flight safety incident will be treated as a failure of duty, regardless of the urgency of the tasking.
4.2Authorisation to Depart▼

Departure requires either a tasking request from ground command, a self-deployment decision by the crew, or an authorised patrol departure. In all cases:

  • The on-duty Sergeant must be informed of the departure unless it is in direct response to an active radio tasking.
  • The MDT departure log must be completed before wheels-up.
  • If departing for routine patrol rather than a specific tasking, the pilot must declare a patrol area or route.
4.3Weather & Visibility Assessment▼

The pilot has sole authority to make the decision on whether conditions are safe to fly. No ground officer or supervisor can override a pilot's assessment that conditions are unsafe.

  • If visibility is significantly reduced — heavy fog, storm conditions — the pilot must stand down and inform Comms.
  • Night operations are permitted provided the aircraft spotlight and crew NVG (night vision) capability are functional.
  • The pilot must reassess conditions continuously throughout the flight and land immediately if conditions deteriorate beyond safe limits.
4.4Refuelling & Turnaround▼

When returning to base for refuelling or crew change, the crew must:

  1. Declare over radio: "I-99 returning to base, [reason] — estimating [time] until back on station."
  2. Hand over any active tracking or tasking to ground units verbally before RTB.
  3. Log the return time on the MDT and note any outstanding taskings.
  4. Complete the turnaround check before departing again — treat every departure as a fresh pre-flight.
05 ✈️

Flight Rules

🚫
These flight rules are non-negotiable. Breaching altitude limits, restricted airspace rules, or reckless flying are serious safety violations and will result in immediate suspension from air operations and a professional standards review.
5.1Minimum Altitude — General Rule▼

The NPAS helicopter must maintain a minimum altitude of 300 feet above ground level (AGL) at all times during normal operations. This applies over all terrain — urban, rural, coastal, and mountainous.

🚫
Flying below 300 feet AGL without an emergency or operational justification is a flight safety violation. This rule exists to protect persons on the ground, property, and the aircraft itself from collision risk.

The 300-foot minimum may only be temporarily reduced for:

  • A controlled landing approach to a helipad or designated landing site.
  • An emergency landing due to technical failure or crew emergency.
  • A search and rescue operation where ground-level observation is operationally essential and the area has been confirmed clear of hazards.

Any operation below 300 feet must be declared to Comms immediately and logged on the MDT with justification.

5.2Altitude Bands & Operational Guidance▼

The following altitude bands are used as operational guidance for different mission types. These are not hard limits above 300 feet — the pilot uses professional judgement — but serve as best-practice benchmarks.

300–500 ft AGL
Close Surveillance / Search
Active MISPER ground search, close tracking of a slow-moving subject on foot. Low noise, high visibility to subject. Use sparingly and declare to Comms.
500–1,000 ft AGL
Active Pursuit / Tracking
Standard pursuit support and vehicle tracking. Effective camera range, covert presence. Most common operational altitude band.
1,000–1,500 ft AGL
Area Patrol / Scene Overview
Routine patrol, scene monitoring, wide-area search. Maximum covert capability — aircraft difficult to hear from ground level.
1,500 ft+ AGL
High Patrol / Transit
Transit between locations, wide-area observation, or when no active tasking requires lower altitude. Reduced camera effectiveness at extreme range.
5.3Restricted Airspace & No-Fly Zones▼

The following areas are designated restricted or no-fly zones within the City of Orbit. The aircraft must not enter these areas without explicit authorisation from Silver Command or above:

  • Hospital airspace — the area directly above and within 200 metres of the hospital helipad is restricted to medical air traffic only.
  • Active firearms cordons — I-99 must not fly directly over an active firearms incident unless specifically requested by the AFO on scene and authorised by the on-duty Inspector or above.
  • Prison / secure facility airspace — no overflights of correctional or secure government facilities without prior authorisation.
  • Civilian airfields — if the map contains a civilian airport or airfield, I-99 must not enter the immediate approach/departure corridors of that facility.
5.4Speed & Manoeuvring▼
  • Speed must be appropriate for the task. Tracking a slow-moving vehicle requires reduced speed; transit between locations may be at full cruise.
  • Aggressive or reckless manoeuvring — sharp banking, low-level dives, deliberate loss of altitude — is prohibited outside of genuine emergency situations.
  • The aircraft must never be used to buzz, intimidate, or harass persons on the ground — this constitutes misuse of a police asset.
  • Hovering for extended periods at low altitude over a residential area is to be avoided where possible — use slow-speed tracking circuits instead.
5.5Emergency Landing Protocol▼

In the event of a technical failure, medical emergency aboard the aircraft, or any situation requiring immediate landing:

  1. Declare immediately over radio: "I-99 MAYDAY, MAYDAY — [nature of emergency], emergency landing at [location]."
  2. Select the nearest safe landing zone — a flat, open surface clear of persons and obstacles.
  3. Ground units must respond to the declared landing site — treat as a State 0 emergency for crew safety.
  4. On landing, ensure crew are safe before addressing the aircraft. Declare safe landing over radio.
  5. The aircraft must not be re-flown after an emergency landing until it has been assessed and cleared.
🔴
An I-99 MAYDAY declaration takes absolute priority over all other radio traffic. Every unit must clear the channel immediately and respond.
5.6Night Operations▼

Night operations are authorised provided the aircraft is equipped with a functioning spotlight and the crew are comfortable operating in low-light conditions. The following additional rules apply at night:

  • The minimum altitude rule of 300 feet AGL is strictly enforced — terrain is harder to judge visually at night.
  • Spotlight use must be proportionate — do not illuminate residential windows unnecessarily or hold the beam on a person who has complied.
  • FLIR (thermal imaging) is the primary tracking tool at night. Camera operators should switch to thermal as the default mode in low light.
  • If at any point the pilot is unable to maintain safe situational awareness due to darkness or obscured terrain, altitude must be increased immediately.
06 🎙️

Air-to-Ground Communications

6.1Radio Channel Discipline▼

I-99 monitors and transmits on the main Response channel by default. During active taskings, the crew may be moved to a dedicated tactical channel by Comms.

  • Always identify as I-99 before transmitting.
  • Keep transmissions brief and information-focused — avoid unnecessary chat over the air channel.
  • During a live pursuit, I-99 owns the radio channel for commentary. Ground units should hold transmissions unless urgent.
  • If moved to a tactical channel, the handover must be declared on the main channel first: "I-99 moving to TAC 2 for the ongoing."
6.2Airborne Commentary Format▼

When providing live commentary from the air — especially during pursuits and surveillance — transmissions should follow a consistent format so ground units can act immediately:

📢
Standard commentary format:

"I-99 — [vehicle/subject description], [current location], [direction of travel], [speed estimate], [behaviour / notable observations]."

Example:
"I-99 — dark blue BMW, now on Moorfield Road heading north towards the junction, speed approximately 60 miles per hour, driver is making erratic lane changes, single occupant."
  • Update commentary every 10–15 seconds during a fast-moving pursuit.
  • Call any change of direction, vehicle change, or foot bail immediately — these are the most critical updates for ground units.
  • If the subject is lost visually, declare immediately: "I-99 — visual lost, last seen [location] heading [direction]." Do not go silent.
6.3Directing Ground Units▼

When I-99 is on station and has eyes on the subject, the crew may direct ground units to positions for containment, intercept, or arrest.

  • Directions must be clear and use recognisable landmarks, road names, or compass directions.
  • I-99 should place units ahead of the subject, not behind — anticipate the route, don't chase it.
  • When directing units to a foot bail location: "I-99 — subject has bailed on foot, heading east through the car park behind [landmark]. Unit required at [exit point]."
  • Confirm when a ground unit has a visual on the subject: "Unit has visual, I-99 maintain overwatch."
6.4Declaring On Station & Standing Down▼

6.5MDT Usage from the Air▼

The TFO is responsible for MDT usage during flight. The pilot must not operate the MDT whilst the aircraft is in motion or at low altitude — this is a flight safety rule.

  • PNC checks on vehicles observed from the air should be run by the TFO immediately.
  • All taskings attended must be linked in the MDT log in real time where possible.
  • In solo operations, MDT use by the pilot is only permitted when in a stable hover or safely on the ground.
07 🔦

Operational Support

7.1Surveillance & Covert Tracking▼

I-99 is highly effective as a covert surveillance platform. When conducting covert tracking:

  • Operate at 1,000 feet or above to reduce auditory signature — the aircraft becomes very difficult to hear from ground level at this altitude.
  • Do not use the spotlight during covert operations — it immediately betrays the aircraft's position.
  • Avoid flying a direct circle above the subject — use offset orbiting patterns to reduce the chance of the subject looking up and identifying surveillance.
  • Camera and FLIR systems must be used in preference to spotlight wherever possible.
  • If the subject appears to have identified the aircraft, inform ground units immediately — covert advantage is lost and tactics must adapt.
7.2Spotlight Use▼

The aerial spotlight is a powerful tool but must be used proportionately. Indiscriminate spotlight use compromises covert operations and is intrusive to uninvolved members of the public.

  • Spotlight is appropriate during night foot pursuits to illuminate a fleeing subject for ground units.
  • Spotlight may be used to illuminate a search area during a MISPER or suspect search.
  • Do not illuminate residential properties, windows, or gardens unless a specific subject is believed to be in that location.
  • When a subject has been detained, turn off the spotlight promptly — it is no longer necessary and is uncomfortable and distressing to shine on someone who is compliant.
  • Spotlight use must be noted in the MDT flight log.
7.3MISPER & Suspect Search from the Air▼

I-99 is one of the most effective tools for locating missing persons or outstanding suspects over a wide area. When conducting an aerial search:

  1. Obtain a full subject description from ground units before beginning the search — clothing colour is the most important visual identifier from altitude.
  2. Agree a search pattern with ground units — I-99 typically covers outer areas while ground units focus closer to the last known location.
  3. Use FLIR (thermal) as the primary search tool in low light or dense foliage — body heat signatures cut through cover that optical cameras cannot penetrate.
  4. When a possible subject is located, do not immediately spotlight or transmit location loudly — confirm visual first, then advise ground units on a tactical channel where possible.
  5. Maintain the subject in view until a ground unit has confirmed contact or detention.
7.4Scene Overview & Cordon Support▼

At major incidents, I-99 can provide the Scene Commander with a bird's-eye view that is impossible from ground level. This includes:

  • Confirming cordon coverage — identifying gaps that need to be filled by ground units.
  • Monitoring crowd or vehicle movement around the outer cordon perimeter.
  • Identifying approach routes for incoming emergency services.
  • Confirming suspect positions during a firearms or hostage incident — this intelligence is provided to the Scene Commander on a designated tactical channel.
⚠️
During firearms incidents, I-99 must not fly directly over the subject's position at low altitude — this may provoke a response. Maintain a safe altitude and use optical zoom to observe. The aircraft is not armoured.
7.5Evidence Capture from the Air▼

Footage and imagery captured by the NPAS camera system constitutes official police evidence and is subject to the same standards as BWV.

  • All footage must be logged in the MDT with the corresponding incident CAD reference.
  • Camera footage of a pursuit, suspect identification, or incident scene must not be deleted or overwritten — it must be preserved for the relevant investigation or prosecution.
  • If footage is of evidential value, it must be flagged to CID or the investigating officer as soon as possible after the flight.
  • The TFO is responsible for ensuring footage is correctly saved and referenced in the post-flight log.
08 🏎️

Pursuit Support

ℹ️
When I-99 is overhead and has eyes on a pursuit, it assumes the role of primary tactical intelligence provider. Ground units should hold their commentary and defer to I-99 — the aerial view is almost always superior to any ground position.
8.1Taking Over a Pursuit▼

When I-99 arrives at an active pursuit:

  1. Declare on station: "I-99 on station, I have visual on the vehicle."
  2. Ground Primary unit acknowledges and reduces proximity — they no longer need to maintain close pursuit to keep visual contact.
  3. I-99 takes over all running commentary. Ground units go quiet on the channel unless they have urgent information.
  4. Direct ground units to optimal positions — ahead of the vehicle, at likely exit points, or to set up a containment.
  5. If the vehicle stops and subjects flee on foot, provide immediate direction to ground units for the foot bail.
8.2Creating Ground Distance▼

One of the most powerful advantages of air support is the ability to reduce the risk of a vehicle pursuit by creating ground distance between police vehicles and the suspect.

  • Once I-99 has a confirmed visual, the Primary ground unit may be instructed to back off significantly — removing the cat-and-mouse dynamic that causes suspects to drive dangerously.
  • With I-99 maintaining track at altitude, ground units can approach ahead of the vehicle to set up a containment rather than chasing from behind.
  • This technique significantly reduces risk to the public and to officers.
  • The decision to create ground distance is made by I-99 in coordination with the Traffic Unit or highest rank coordinating the pursuit.
8.3Pursuit Termination from the Air▼

I-99 may recommend abandonment of a vehicle pursuit to ground units when, from the air, the risk to the public is clearly unacceptable. The Traffic Unit coordinator or highest ranking officer on scene retains the authority to terminate, but I-99's risk assessment from altitude carries significant weight.

If the pursuit is terminated, I-99 continues to track the vehicle covertly from altitude, providing direction to units that are holding back. The aim is to follow the vehicle to a stop point rather than chase it.

8.4Foot Bail Management▼

Foot bails are one of the most chaotic moments in any pursuit. I-99 is uniquely effective at maintaining track through foot bails:

  • Call the foot bail the moment it occurs: "I-99 — vehicle has stopped, [number] subjects bailing on foot, [location], [direction of travel for each subject]."
  • Prioritise the highest-risk subject if multiple bail simultaneously — e.g. the driver, or anyone carrying something.
  • Track continuously through gardens, car parks, and narrow routes that ground units struggle to navigate at speed.
  • When a subject stops moving or hides, update ground units with precise location: "I-99 — subject is static, hiding behind [landmark], ground unit approach from [direction]."
09 🔴

Incident Response

9.1Responding to a State 0 / Officer Down▼

A State 0 declaration by any ground officer is an immediate priority tasking for I-99. All other taskings are suspended.

  1. Respond immediately to the declared location. Acknowledge over radio: "I-99 received State 0, en route."
  2. On arrival, provide an immediate scene assessment to Comms — how many persons are visible, is the threat still active, where is the downed officer.
  3. Track any suspect(s) fleeing the scene. Officer safety takes priority — I-99 stays on the threat rather than the scene.
  4. Direct responding ground units using live commentary until they are on scene.
9.2Major Incidents & Firearms▼

At a declared major incident or firearms event, I-99 provides overwatch and intelligence to the Scene Commander and AFO units.

  • Maintain a safe altitude above the incident — do not fly low over an active firearms scene.
  • Transmit suspect positions and movements on the tactical channel designated by the Scene Commander.
  • Do not spotlight the area without approval from the AFO on scene — light may affect officer safety in a firearms situation.
  • If the subject attempts to flee, track and provide running commentary immediately.
9.3Emergency Landing at a Scene▼

In exceptional circumstances, I-99 may land at a scene — for example, to provide immediate first aid to a critically injured officer or civilian where HEMS is delayed.

  • An emergency scene landing must be declared to Comms before touchdown.
  • The landing zone must be confirmed clear by ground units before I-99 descends.
  • Once on the ground, crew operate under the authority of the Scene Commander.
  • The aircraft must be secured and guarded — it must not be left unattended at any scene.
  • Crew must re-don hi-vis over their flight suit before exiting the aircraft at any scene.
9.4Critical Incident Notification▼

If I-99 observes or becomes aware of any of the following from the air, it must immediately notify Comms and the on-duty Sergeant, regardless of whether a ground unit is already responding:

  • A vehicle collision with serious casualties.
  • A person in the water, on a rooftop, or in a location suggesting imminent self-harm.
  • A group assault or serious public disorder.
  • A fire or explosion.
  • Any incident where the crew assess life to be at immediate risk.

I-99 does not have to be formally tasked to report what it observes — the obligation to report is unconditional.

10 📝

Post-Flight & Logging

10.1Post-Flight Procedure▼

Upon landing at base at the end of a deployment, the crew must complete the following before standing down:

  1. Declare over radio: "I-99 on the ground at [location], standing down."
  2. Secure the aircraft — engines off, aircraft secured at the hangar/helipad.
  3. Complete the MDT flight log — record departure time, total flight time, all taskings attended, and any notable observations.
  4. Flag any footage of evidential value to the relevant case officer or CID.
  5. Note any technical faults or equipment issues observed during the flight in the aircraft log — do not leave a fault unreported.
  6. Return all personal equipment to the appropriate storage.
10.2MDT Flight Log Requirements▼

Every flight must have a corresponding MDT log entry. The log must include:

  • Pilot name, collar number, and Air ticket reference.
  • TFO name and collar number (if crewed).
  • Departure time, landing time, and total flight duration.
  • All incidents attended — CAD references must be linked.
  • Whether spotlight was used, and if so, for what purpose.
  • Whether footage was captured that is of evidential value.
  • Any altitude deviations below 300 feet and the justification.
  • Any technical faults or equipment defects observed.
10.3Debrief Following Significant Operations▼

Following any flight that involved a critical incident, a State 0 response, a firearms operation, or a significant pursuit, the NPAS crew must attend or contribute to the post-incident debrief alongside ground units. Air unit observations from altitude often contain intelligence that ground units were not aware of — this information must be shared in the debrief.

11 🚫

Restrictions & Misconduct

11.1Prohibited Conduct — Absolute Rules▼

The following actions are absolutely prohibited and will result in immediate suspension from air operations and a full professional standards review:

  • Operating the aircraft without a valid Air training ticket.
  • Flying below 300 feet AGL outside of authorised exceptions.
  • Carrying an unauthorised passenger — civilian or otherwise.
  • Using the aircraft or its equipment for any purpose that is not a legitimate police function.
  • Operating the aircraft in incorrect or non-standard uniform.
  • Deliberately using the spotlight to harass, intimidate, or torment a member of the public.
  • Flying under the influence of anything that impairs judgement or reaction time.
  • Failing to declare an emergency MAYDAY when one exists — delaying a distress call endangers crew and ground units.
  • Deliberately concealing a flight safety incident, fault, or near-miss from the post-flight log.
11.2Misuse of the Aircraft▼

The NPAS helicopter is a publicly-funded operational asset. It exists solely to support police operations. The following are considered misuse and are subject to disciplinary action:

  • Using the aircraft for personal sightseeing or joy rides — including for friends, family, or other officers not on duty.
  • Engaging in aerial stunts, reckless manoeuvring, or low-level flying for entertainment purposes.
  • Using the aircraft to follow or surveil persons without a legitimate policing basis.
  • Flying over known events or gatherings purely for spectacle rather than an operational reason.
11.3Reporting Flight Safety Concerns▼

Any pilot or observer who has a concern about flight safety — whether related to equipment, another crew member's conduct, or a near-miss incident — has an unconditional duty to report it to a Sergeant or above.

  • Flight safety concerns must never be suppressed due to rank, social pressure, or fear of embarrassment.
  • Reports can be made confidentially to the on-duty Sergeant — the reporting officer will be protected from retaliation.
  • Near-miss incidents must be logged on the MDT and flagged to an Inspector or above on the same shift.
✅
Reporting a safety concern is correct and professional behaviour. The only failure is in staying silent. An unreported fault or near-miss today could cause a serious incident tomorrow.
11.4Ticket Suspension & Revocation▼

An Air ticket may be suspended or permanently revoked by a Superintendent or above in the following circumstances:

  • A flight safety incident deemed to be caused by the officer's conduct or negligence.
  • A serious breach of these SOPs — particularly the 300-foot rule, uniform requirements, or prohibited conduct.
  • An ongoing professional standards investigation where the use of air operations is relevant.
  • A sustained period of inactivity where currency of flight skills cannot be confirmed.

Suspension is reviewed after a defined period or following a satisfactory requalification assessment. Revocation is permanent unless overturned by a Superintendent or above on appeal.
